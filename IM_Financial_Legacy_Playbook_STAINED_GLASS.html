A Word Before We Begin

"A good man leaves an inheritance to his children's children, but the wealth of the sinner is stored up for the righteous."

— Proverbs 13:22 (NKJV)

This is not a financial seminar. This is a stewardship reckoning.

The Intentional Men movement was built on a singular truth: every decision made today is either building or destroying what will be left behind for the next generation. Financial wealth is the FIRST Wealth Stream not because money is supreme, but because the absence of financial systems creates pressure that bleeds into every other stream — spiritual, relational, intellectual, and time.

This playbook exists because Black men in America are building on ground that was deliberately disrupted. The racial wealth gap is not a motivation gap. It is a systemic gap — one that demands intentional, disciplined, Spirit-led response.

"For which of you, intending to build a tower, does not sit down first and count the cost, whether he has enough to finish it?"

— Luke 14:28 (NKJV)

This playbook is the cost-counting. Let's begin.

Part One: Understanding the Battlefield

The Racial Wealth Gap

The numbers are not comfortable. The median Black household holds approximately $44,100 in net worth compared to $284,310 for the median White household — about 15 cents for every dollar. This gap did not emerge from personal failures. It was constructed through generations of policy, exclusion, and denied access to wealth-building tools that other communities took for granted.

But Proverbs 13:22 does not say "a good man complains about the wealth of the sinner." It says the wealth stored up for the wicked is transferred to the righteous. That transfer does not happen passively. It happens when intentional men build systems, protect assets, and refuse to let another generation start from zero.

"Do you see a man who excels in his work? He will stand before kings; he will not stand before unknown men."

— Proverbs 22:29 (NKJV)

Why Wealth Is Different from Income

A strong income without a wealth strategy is a fast car with no destination. Income flows through the hands. Wealth is what remains — what compounds, what protects, and what transfers.

Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities

A man can earn $80,000 a year and have a net worth of zero. Another man earning $45,000 — with systems, protection, and equity — can have a net worth building steadily toward financial legacy. The difference is intentionality.

Part Two: The Wealth-Building Hierarchy

"Wisdom is the principal thing; therefore get wisdom. And in all your getting, get understanding."

— Proverbs 4:7 (NKJV)

Wisdom means building in the right order. Attempting to invest aggressively before the financial foundation is stable is like planting seeds on a roof — there is no ground for roots.

  1. Spiritual and Personal Stewardship — Align every money decision with character, discipline, and Kingdom purpose.
  2. Cash-Flow Clarity — A written budget is liberation. Every dollar given a name stops wandering.
  3. Starter Reserves — Build an emergency buffer so a car repair does not become a credit card crisis.
  4. Debt Stabilization — Stop high-interest debt from consuming the margin God entrusted to you.
  5. Core Protection — Insurance, legal documents, and beneficiaries are stewardship essentials.
  6. Credit Optimization — Strong credit reduces the cost of building.
  7. Ownership Strategy — Build equity through retirement accounts, investing, homeownership, and business value.
  8. Legacy Transfer — Estate plans, succession strategies, and family financial discipleship.

"The plans of the diligent lead surely to plenty, but those of everyone who is hasty, surely to poverty."

— Proverbs 21:5 (NKJV)

Part Four: The Budget — Stewarding What God Gave

"Honor the LORD with your possessions, and with the firstfruits of all your increase."

— Proverbs 3:9 (NKJV)

Honoring God with possessions begins with knowing what the possessions are and where they go. The 50/30/20 framework is a starting diagnostic — not a law, but a lens.

BucketTargetCovers
Needs~50%Housing, utilities, food, transportation, insurance, minimum debt payments
Wants~30%Entertainment, dining, travel, hobbies, discretionary giving
Savings & Investing~20%Emergency fund, retirement contributions, extra debt paydown, investments

"For the love of money is a root of all kinds of evil, for which some have strayed from the faith in their greediness, and pierced themselves through with many sorrows."

— 1 Timothy 6:10 (NKJV)

The NKJV is precise: it is the love of money, not money itself, that destroys. A man with a budget is not chasing money. He is directing it.

Part Five: Protection — The Armor of Financial Stewardship

"A prudent man foresees evil and hides himself, but the simple pass on and are punished."

— Proverbs 22:3 (NKJV)

  • Health Insurance — Reduces exposure to the most common cause of financial catastrophe in American households.
  • Term Life Insurance — It is a promise kept to your family even after death.
  • Disability Insurance — The ability to earn is often a household's largest asset. Protect it.
  • Homeowners or Renters Insurance — Property and liability protection.
  • Auto Insurance — Legal requirement and practical protection.
  • Umbrella Liability Insurance — For households with growing assets, income, or landlord responsibilities.

"By wisdom a house is built, and by understanding it is established; by knowledge the rooms are filled with all precious and pleasant riches."

— Proverbs 24:3–4 (NKJV)

"The rich rules over the poor, and the borrower is servant to the lender."

— Proverbs 22:7 (NKJV)

Homeownership transitions a man from renter — building a landlord's equity — to owner, building his own. Equity compounds quietly. Equity transfers generationally.

Part Seven: Business & Tax Strategy — Ownership Changes the Math

"She considers a field and buys it; from her profits she plants a vineyard."

— Proverbs 31:16 (NKJV)

Business equity is one of the most powerful accelerators of Black family wealth. Assess the opportunity, acquire when ready, and reinvest with discipline.

  • Separate personal and business finances from day one.
  • Build bookkeeping discipline before it is urgently needed.
  • Pay yourself intentionally, not randomly.
  • Track profit margin, owner compensation, tax reserve, and cash runway.
  • Build systems so the business can function beyond the founder.

"The soul of a lazy man desires, and has nothing; but the soul of the diligent shall be made rich."

— Proverbs 13:4 (NKJV)

Part Eight: Credit Optimization — Borrowing Power as a Tool

"The wicked borrows and does not repay, but the righteous shows mercy and gives."

— Psalm 37:21 (NKJV)

  • Pay every obligation on time, every time.
  • Keep revolving utilization below 30% — lower as a goal.
  • Review credit reports regularly for errors or fraud.
  • Avoid unnecessary hard inquiries.
  • Never use a credit card as a substitute for income margin that does not exist.

Part Nine: Investing — Making Money Work While You Pray

"Cast your bread upon the waters, for you will find it after many days."

— Ecclesiastes 11:1 (NKJV)

Compound interest allows money to earn returns on prior returns. When $100 earns 5% annually, it becomes $105 after year one — and $110.25 after year two, because interest is earned on prior interest. The earlier the seed is planted, the longer the compound growth runs.

"Divide your investments among many places, for you do not know what risks might lie ahead."

— Ecclesiastes 11:2 (NKJV)

Diversification is not a modern portfolio theory concept. It is Solomonic wisdom.

  1. Build and maintain emergency savings.
  2. Capture any employer retirement match — immediate 100% return on that portion.
  3. Eliminate toxic high-interest debt.
  4. Add regular, consistent retirement contributions.
  5. Consider diversified long-term brokerage investing.
  6. Increase contributions as income grows.

Part Eleven: Accountability Covenant

"As iron sharpens iron, so a man sharpens the countenance of his friend."

— Proverbs 27:17 (NKJV)

The IM Financial Covenant

  • Review my budget monthly.
  • Calculate my net worth quarterly.
  • Increase my savings rate intentionally and consistently.
  • Avoid new consumer debt unless strategically necessary.
  • Maintain appropriate insurance coverage at all times.
  • Complete or update estate documents within this calendar year.
  • Share one measurable financial win and one honest challenge with my accountability partner each month.
  • Steward what God entrusted to me as if it belongs to Him — because it does.

"Two are better than one, because they have a good reward for their labor. For if they fall, one will lift up his companion."
